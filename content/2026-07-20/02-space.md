title: Psyche used Mars as a practice target on its way to a metal world
kicker: Space
author: The Editors
summary: NASA released data and video from the Psyche spacecraft's May flyby of Mars, a dress rehearsal for its 2029 arrival at a metal-rich asteroid.
order: 2
---

NASA's Psyche spacecraft is headed for a metal-rich asteroid of the
same name, out in the belt between Mars and Jupiter. It won't get
there until 2029. Along the way, in mid-May, it swung past Mars for a
gravity assist — and mission scientists used the close pass to put the
spacecraft's instruments through a live rehearsal, releasing the data
and a time-lapse video of the encounter this past week
([NASA Science](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/psyche/2026/07/17/nasas-psyche-mission-delivers-mars-flyby-data-time-lapse-video/)).

Psyche came within 2,864 miles of the Martian surface. Its
multispectral imager, led by Arizona State University's Jim Bell,
caught detailed views of windblown craters, the south polar ice cap,
and Huygens crater. The gamma-ray and neutron spectrometer, led by
Johns Hopkins APL's David Lawrence, picked up the expected rise in
neutron counts, while its magnetometer, run by MIT's Ben Weiss,
measured the solar wind slamming into Mars's magnetic field at the
planet's bow shock.

"The mission's imager, magnetometer, and gamma-ray and neutron
spectrometer teams worked overtime to make full use of this planetary
encounter," said principal investigator Lindy Elkins-Tanton. Project
manager Bob Mase reports the spacecraft is "in great shape," with its
solar-electric propulsion set to resume thrusting this fall to keep it
on course.

It's a modest kind of milestone — no discovery, just instruments doing
exactly what they were built to do, on a planet the mission wasn't
even going to. But calibration done well at Mars is calibration you
don't have to worry about when the far stranger target, a world made
largely of iron and nickel, finally comes into view.
