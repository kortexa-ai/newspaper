---
title: Solar became the EU's single biggest power source, for the third month ever
kicker: Climate
author: The Editors
summary: A quarter of all EU electricity in June came from the sun, according to new data from energy think tank Ember, edging out nuclear, gas, and wind combined.
order: 3
---

For the first time, solar power supplied a full quarter of the European
Union's electricity in a single month. Ember, the energy think tank that
tracks the bloc's power mix, reported that solar generated a record 52
terawatt-hours in June, enough to claim 25% of EU generation and make it
the bloc's single largest power source for the month — ahead of nuclear
(21%), gas (15%), wind (14%), hydro (12%), and coal, which supplied just
8%
([Ember](https://ember-energy.org/latest-insights/a-quarter-of-eu-power-came-from-solar-for-the-first-time-in-june/);
corroborated by
[Electrek](https://electrek.co/2026/07/15/solar-just-became-europes-biggest-source-of-electricity-heres-the-milestone-it-hit/)
and
[CleanTechnica](https://cleantechnica.com/2026/07/14/25-of-eu-power-came-from-solar-for-the-first-time-in-june/)).

It's only the third month on record — after June 2025 and May of this
year — that solar has topped every other source across the EU, and the
trajectory behind it is steep: in June 2021, solar generated just 21 TWh
and a 10% share. Individual countries are moving even faster than the
bloc-wide average. Spain drew 34% of its power from solar in June, a
national first; Germany reached 36%; and Poland, which has added more
than 20 gigawatts of solar capacity since 2020, hit 24%.

"Solar's rise has been truly stratospheric, beating prediction after
prediction," said Chris Rosslowe, senior analyst at Ember. "In just a few
years solar has gone from a small player to an essential part of Europe's
power system."

Summer sun makes June a favorable month for solar by nature, and the
share will fall back as days shorten. But the pattern across 2026 — with
eighteen EU countries setting new monthly solar records so far this year
— points less to a seasonal blip than to a power system being steadily
rebuilt around the cheapest new electricity there is.
