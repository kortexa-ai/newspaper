Red dwarf stars are not gentle hosts. They flare, they blast their
close-orbiting planets with radiation and stellar wind, and over
billions of years that tends to strip a rocky world down to bare rock —
which is part of why the search for habitable planets around the
galaxy's most common stars has stayed mostly theoretical. This week it
got a data point.

Collin Cherubim, who recently completed his Ph.D. at Harvard, led a
team that detected helium escaping from LHS 1140 b, a rocky planet
orbiting a red dwarf 48 light-years away — the first confirmed
atmosphere on a rocky world sitting inside another star's habitable
zone. The result was published July 16 in *Science*
([Center for Astrophysics | Harvard & Smithsonian](https://www.cfa.harvard.edu/news/first-atmosphere-detected-habitable-zone-rocky-world);
corroborated by
[EurekAlert](https://www.eurekalert.org/news-releases/1135901) and
[phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2026-07-life-solar-atmosphere-habitable-zone.html)).

Cherubim didn't stumble onto the atmosphere — he predicted it. His
theoretical model suggested LHS 1140 b should have an upper atmosphere
rich in helium, slowly leaking into space, and that the leak would be
detectable if astronomers caught the right moment. His joint advisor,
Harvard astronomy department chair David Charbonneau, was skeptical
at first: the prediction rested on a calculation, for a kind of
detection nobody had made on a rocky world before. "Collin analyzed the
planets we knew about and predicted that this one would have a helium
atmosphere," Charbonneau said. "The detection was statistically
rock-solid."

The team got their moment using the Warm Infrared Echelle (WINERED)
Spectrograph on the Magellan Telescope at Las Campanas Observatory in
Chile, during a rare night when LHS 1140 b and a second planet in the
same system happened to transit their star simultaneously. The
companion planet showed no sign of an atmosphere at all. LHS 1140 b
did — helium, still bleeding off into space after more than three
billion years of orbiting a hostile little star, which is itself the
finding: not just that the planet has an atmosphere now, but that
something about it has let that atmosphere survive.

"An atmosphere is essential for a planet to support life as we know
it," Cherubim said. "This is the first time anyone has found an
atmosphere on a rocky planet in the habitable zone of another star."

Nobody is claiming LHS 1140 b is inhabited, or even necessarily
habitable — a helium-rich upper atmosphere escaping into space is a
long way from evidence of liquid water or biology. What changes is the
odds. Red dwarfs make up roughly three-quarters of the stars in the
galaxy, and until now the working assumption was that their habitable
zones were mostly stripped bare. One confirmed atmosphere, found
exactly where a model said to look, is reason to keep looking — at LHS
1140 b in more detail, and at the long list of similar worlds waiting
their turn.
