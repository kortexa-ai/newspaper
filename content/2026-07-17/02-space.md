---
title: Serbia becomes the 69th nation to sign the Artemis Accords
kicker: Space
author: The Editors
summary: Serbia's foreign minister signed the international framework for peaceful lunar and space exploration at a ceremony in Washington.
order: 2
---

Serbia's Minister of Foreign Affairs, Marko Đurić, signed the Artemis
Accords on July 16 at a ceremony hosted by NASA at its Mary W. Jackson
headquarters in Washington, making Serbia the 69th country to join the
framework since it launched in 2020
([NASA](https://www.nasa.gov/organizations/oiir/artemis-accords/nasa-welcomes-serbia-as-newest-artemis-accords-signatory/)).
NASA Deputy Administrator Matt Anderson attended on the agency's behalf.

The Accords are a set of non-binding principles for how nations conduct
themselves in space and on the Moon: exploring peacefully and
transparently, sharing scientific data, helping other signatories in
distress, avoiding harmful interference with others' operations, and
preserving historically significant sites like Apollo landing zones. They
don't carry the force of a treaty, but they've become the practical
common ground a growing list of spacefaring and space-curious nations
have signed onto.

For Serbia, the signing formalizes a relationship with NASA that traces
back further than most: Serbian-born engineers contributed to the Apollo
program decades before Belgrade had a space agency of its own. Joining
now opens the door to more concrete collaboration going forward, including
opportunities to contribute science and technology payloads to NASA's
planned lunar Moon Base and CubeSats for upcoming Artemis missions.

"We owe it to both our brave ancestors and our children to keep pushing
toward new frontiers — to explore, to inspire one another," Đurić said at
the ceremony.

Sixty-nine signatories is a long way from universal, but the list has
grown steadily and now spans every inhabited continent — a sign that, for
all the geopolitical friction space exploration can generate, the basic
etiquette of sharing it peacefully still has broad appeal.
