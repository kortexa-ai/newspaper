Most frontier AI is built by a handful of companies chasing the
biggest markets first, which means the languages and communities
outside those markets tend to wait. Current AI, a nonprofit founded in
February 2025, is trying to build the alternative: open, public AI
infrastructure aimed squarely at the people proprietary systems
under-serve ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/19/nonprofit-current-ai-is-racing-to-build-the-world-wide-web-of-ai-free-for-all/)).

The organization, led by CEO Ayah Bdeir — formerly of Mozilla's AI
strategy team and founder of the education company littleBits — has
lined up $400 million in committed funding, including $100 million in
seed money from the French government plus contributions from the Ford
Foundation, the MacArthur Foundation, DeepMind, and Salesforce.
"Funders, not investors," as Bdeir puts it. The money is already moving:
a partnership with India's Bhashini division produced Suno Sutra, an
offline device running AI across 22 Indian languages, and a coalition
including Hugging Face, Mozilla, and MIT Media Lab assembled AlphaChat,
an open-source chatbot, in seven weeks. A separate $3.2 million in
grants went to four organizations in Kenya, Lebanon, and Brazil working
on farming, education, and Indigenous knowledge preservation.

It's a modest budget next to what any single frontier lab spends on
compute in a year. But infrastructure built to be public rather than
proprietary doesn't need to out-scale the giants to matter — it just
needs to exist for the people the giants aren't building for.
