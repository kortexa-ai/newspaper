---
title: Feynman's sprinkler puzzle is finally settled, a century on
kicker: Science
author: The Editors
summary: NYU mathematicians built working rigs to answer a question about spinning sprinklers that had gone unresolved since the 1880s.
order: 5
---

Point a lawn sprinkler's arms so the water shoots out one way, and it
spins the other way — simple enough. But run it backward, sucking water
in instead of spraying it out, and physicists have argued for well over
a century about which way, if any, it would turn. Richard Feynman
picked up the puzzle in the 1980s and never fully resolved it either.

A team at NYU's Courant Institute, led by Professor Leif Ristroph with
Jesse Etan Smith, Mingxuan Zuo, Will Kuhlke, and Brennan Sprinkle,
finally settled it the old-fashioned way: they built the sprinklers and
watched. Publishing in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of
Sciences* on July 15
([NYU Courant](https://math.nyu.edu/dynamic/news/99/); corroborated by
[ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260715083535.htm)
and
[phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2026-07-silly-sprinklers-reverse-unravel-decades.html)),
they found that a reverse, or "suction," sprinkler does rotate — in the
opposite direction from a normal one — but far more slowly, and the
effect holds even for oddly shaped arms. The rotation, in both
directions, turns out to be governed by the angular momentum of the
water jets themselves as they leave or enter the nozzles.

"This Feynman problem, as put by Feynman — this is solved," the team
said of the result. It's not a discovery that will change how anyone
waters their lawn. But it's a tidy example of how a toy sitting in
plain sight for a hundred and forty years can still hide a real
question, and how sometimes the only way to answer it is to build the
thing and turn on the tap.
