You have found a newspaper. It comes out every morning, it is about
technology, and it prints only good news. That combination is unusual
enough to deserve a word of explanation before you start reading.

## Why

Most coverage of technology runs on dread. Dread is easy — it writes
itself, and it travels fast. But it is not the whole truth. Every single
day, people ship open-source tools that thousands quietly rely on,
decode another corner of a disease, drive the cost of clean energy down
another notch, and build useful things in garages for no better reason
than that they wanted them to exist. That work is real, it compounds,
and it is chronically underreported — not because it doesn't matter,
but because it happens steadily instead of loudly.

This paper is a correction. Not a rose-tinted one: optimism here is a
discipline, not a mood. We don't pretend problems away — we report on
the people solving them.

## How

There is no newsroom, no server, no database, no feed algorithm, no
tracking, and no comments section. Each morning the day's stories are
written as plain markdown files and committed to a public repository.
A small robot notices, rebuilds the site, and publishes it as static
pages. The archive is, quite literally, a folder of days. If the whole
apparatus disappeared tomorrow, the paper would still exist as text
files anyone could read.

## The sky

The page you're reading loads before dawn, and the sun rises as you
scroll. The paper brightens as you read it. That is the entire thesis
of this publication, rendered in pixels.

## What to expect

A handful of stories each morning — one lead, a few shorter pieces, a
few minutes of your time. Past mornings stay browsable at the bottom of
the page. That's it.

Come back tomorrow morning. The sun will be up.
