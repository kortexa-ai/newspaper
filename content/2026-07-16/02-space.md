title: Astronomers find the faintest planet ever imaged, hiding in a decade of old photos
kicker: Space
author: The Editors
summary: A third planet around Beta Pictoris turned up only after researchers dug back through 11 years of archival telescope images that had missed it every time.
order: 2
---

Beta Pictoris, a young star 63 light-years away, has been one of
astronomy's favorite planet-hunting grounds for decades — it's close,
it's bright, and its planets have been directly photographed since 2008.
So it's a little remarkable that a third planet in the system, Beta
Pictoris d, went unnoticed until now, even though the data to find it
had been sitting in telescope archives the whole time.

A team co-led by Ben Sutlieff (University of Edinburgh) and Markus Bonse
(European Southern Observatory) spotted the planet with the Very Large
Telescope's ERIS instrument, then went back through more than a decade
of archival images from the VLT's SPHERE instrument to confirm it was
really there. A separate, independent team led by Aidan Gibbs at the
University of California corroborated the discovery using James Webb
Space Telescope data. The result is published in *The Astrophysical
Journal Letters*
([ESO](https://www.eso.org/public/news/eso2609/)).

The planet is about 2.4 times the mass of Jupiter — and 100 times
fainter than its sibling, Beta Pictoris b, which is why it took so long
to find. "The new planet is 100 times fainter than Beta Pictoris b, the
famous planet in the same system, making it the faintest exoplanet ever
imaged directly from Earth," said Bonse. Sutlieff described the find as
"serendipitous": the team was originally re-examining Beta Pictoris b to
track how it changes over time, not hunting for a new world. Oxford's
Jayne Birkby, a co-author, put it more simply: "Planet d, it seems, has
been playing a game of hide-and-seek with us for over a decade."

The discovery is as much a validation of method as of the planet itself.
Direct imaging — actually photographing a planet, rather than inferring
it from a star's wobble or a transit dimming — is the hardest way to
find exoplanets, and the payoff here came from an old idea: don't just
build a better camera, look harder at what past cameras already
captured. With better processing techniques, over a decade of shelved
data turned out to be enough.
