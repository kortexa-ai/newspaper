title: A quantum computer that runs at room temperature, built to sit in a data center rack
kicker: Science
author: The Editors
summary: Dutch-German firm QuiX Quantum unveiled Carina, a photonic quantum system that skips the cryogenic cooling most quantum computers need and can run any gate-based algorithm, not just a narrow specialty task.

---

Most quantum computers need to be chilled to a fraction of a degree above
absolute zero to work, which is a large part of why they live in
specialized labs rather than ordinary data centers. QuiX Quantum, a
Dutch-German company, announced a photonic system this week, called
Carina, built to avoid that problem entirely
([QuiX Quantum](https://www.quixquantum.com/news/quix-quantum-announces-carina);
corroborated by The Quantum Insider and HPCwire).

Carina uses single photons — particles of light — as its qubits instead
of the superconducting circuits most quantum computers rely on, which
means it operates at room temperature with no cryogenic cooling
required. It also does something earlier photonic systems couldn't:
implement a universal gate set, meaning it can, in principle, run any
gate-based quantum algorithm rather than being limited to a single
specialized task like boson sampling. The system integrates photon
generation, routing, state preparation, measurement, and fast
feed-forward control into one compact unit designed to slot into
standard data-center infrastructure alongside conventional servers.
"Carina marks a major milestone for QuiX and the photonic quantum
computing industry towards deploying utility-scale quantum systems,"
said CEO Stefan Hengesbach.

The system was developed under the Universal Photonic Quantum Computer
project, part of the German Aerospace Center's Quantum Computing
Initiative, funded by Germany's federal research ministry. Whether
photonic, room-temperature quantum computing can match the raw
performance of cryogenic approaches is still an open question the field
will spend years answering. But a design built from the outset to fit
into existing data centers, rather than requiring one built around it,
is a bet on a much more mundane and important problem: getting quantum
hardware out of the physics lab and into places where ordinary
engineers can actually plug it in.
