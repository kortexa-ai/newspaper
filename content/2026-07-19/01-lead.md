Most rockets don't make orbit on their first attempt. Vikram-1 did.

Early on July 18, from the Satish Dhawan Space Centre on India's east
coast, Skyroot Aerospace lit the three solid-fuel stages and liquid
kick-stage of its debut orbital rocket and watched it climb toward a
450-kilometer low Earth orbit — arriving there roughly fifteen minutes
after liftoff, on schedule after a brief 35-minute delay for a technical
check
([SpaceNews](https://spacenews.com/skyroot-aerospace-reaches-orbit-on-first-vikram-1-launch/);
corroborated by
[Space.com](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/launches-spacecraft/skyroot-aerospace-india-first-private-orbital-launch-vikram-1)
and
[CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/18/indias-skyroot-launches-first-private-orbital-rocket-mission.html)).
It was the company's first orbital attempt, and it worked.

That makes Skyroot, a Hyderabad-based startup founded in 2018 by two
former ISRO engineers, the first private Indian company to put a rocket
into orbit — and puts India in a short list of nations, alongside the
United States and China, with a commercial launch provider capable of
reaching space on its own. Vikram-1 carried a real payload manifest for
a maiden flight: two cubesats, one built by Skyroot itself and one from
the Indian startup Grahaa Space, plus hosted technology demonstrations
from Dcubed and Cosmoserve Space. Riding along, in a smaller and more
sentimental gesture, were postcards signed by Indian Prime Minister
Narendra Modi.

"In the first attempt reaching orbit, I never thought it was possible,
but the Skyroot team made it possible," said co-founder and CEO Pawan
Kumar Chandana, calling it "a historic moment not just for Skyroot but
also for India and the entire global space sector."

The achievement is the product of years of quieter groundwork. India
opened its space sector to private launch companies only in 2020, and
Skyroot spent the years since building toward this flight — a 2022
suborbital test of its smaller Vikram-S rocket, followed by years of
engine testing and stage qualification for the orbital-class Vikram-1,
developed with backing from India's Department of Space and its
private-sector liaison, IN-SPACe. Where SpaceX needed several failed
attempts before Falcon 1 first reached orbit in 2008, Skyroot's team
got there in one.

What it unlocks is more prosaic than the fireworks of launch day, and
also more important: a second credible commercial launch option
alongside government-run ISRO missions, for Indian and international
satellite operators who currently have limited choices for getting
small payloads to orbit. Cheaper, more frequent access to space has
historically been the thing that lets other industries — Earth
observation, communications, debris-tracking, materials science in
microgravity — actually grow, rather than staying dependent on a
handful of launch providers with years-long queues. Skyroot says
Vikram-1 is the first of a family of vehicles; on the evidence of this
flight, orbit is no longer the open question.
