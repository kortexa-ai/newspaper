title: Proton 11 lands, and a thousand old games come in from the cold

Proton, the open-source compatibility layer that lets Windows games
run on Linux, shipped version 11.0-1 this week — and the changelog
reads like a love letter to people with long memories. Resident Evil
(1996), Resident Evil 2 (1998), Dino Crisis, and SHOGUN: Total War
graduate from experimental status into the stable release; Gothic 1
Classic, X-Plane 12, Breath of Fire IV, and Deadly Premonition join
the playable list.

The unglamorous fixes matter more. A recent EA App update had broken
a swath of EA games for Linux players; Proton 11 restores them. It
also fixes crashes in HELLDIVERS 2, brings back No Man's Sky VR
support, and rebases the whole stack on Wine 11.0 with updated
graphics components — the plumbing that quietly determines whether
thousands of titles run well on the Steam Deck and on any Linux
desktop.

The larger story is the model. Game compatibility used to be
something you lost when you left Windows, permanently. A decade of
open-source work — Wine, DXVK, Proton, and the community's
patch-by-patch persistence — turned it into something that improves
on a schedule, for everyone, for free. Version 11 is one more
installment of a promise being kept.
