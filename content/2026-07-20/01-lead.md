title: A 600-mile EV road trip, and almost nothing went wrong
kicker: Infrastructure

In 2023, Tim De Chant drove an electric car 350 miles round-trip to
Maine and came home with a story about broken chargers, dead screens,
and three calls to customer service. Last week, TechCrunch's senior
climate reporter made a longer trip — 600 miles from home to Montreal
and back in an Audi e-tron with roughly 220 miles of range per charge —
and came home with almost nothing to complain about
([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/18/a-600-mile-road-trip-and-data-proves-ev-charging-doesnt-suck-anymore/)).

Three charging stops, about twenty minutes each. One hiccup, at a
Circuit Électrique station in Canada, where the card reader wouldn't
take payment — a five-minute annoyance, not a trip-ending one. Every
other charger simply worked, on the first try, as expected.

That's a small, undramatic sentence to build a story around, and that's
rather the point. Public charging has been the most persistent
complaint about EV ownership for years — AAA's most recent survey found
just over half of respondents named it a key concern — and De Chant's
piece backs the anecdote with numbers. Paren, which tracks charger
uptime across the U.S., puts its national reliability index in the
mid-90s now, up from 85 a year ago. The count of DC fast chargers
nationwide has more than doubled since his 2023 trip, helped along by
Tesla opening its Supercharger network to other brands and by federal
and state buildout money finally turning into stations in the ground
rather than plans on paper.

None of this is the kind of progress that makes for a splashy
announcement. Nobody cut a ribbon on "charging that works." It's the
accumulation of thousands of unglamorous decisions — better payment
hardware, more redundant cabling, software that reports outages
instead of hiding them, networks that finally scaled past their early,
undersized builds — adding up to something you only notice in its
absence: a road trip where the car was never the story.

De Chant's own conclusion is the right-sized one: "My nearly flawless
trip last week appears to be the norm, not the exception." Infrastructure
that quietly stops being a problem doesn't trend. It just lets people
get where they're going, which was always the point of building it.
