Solar power's oldest limitation is the one nobody can argue with: the sun
sets. A project that just reached financial close in Abu Dhabi is built
around solving exactly that, at a scale few have attempted.

Masdar, the UAE's state-run clean energy developer, announced this week
that it has closed $6.1 billion in financing for a 5.2-gigawatt solar
array paired with 19-gigawatt-hours of battery storage, developed with
the Emirates Water and Electricity Company. The financing splits into
$5.1 billion in debt from a consortium of 13 local and international
banks, plus $1 billion of Masdar's own equity
([PV-Tech](https://www.pv-tech.org/masdar-financial-close-5-2gw-19gwh-round-the-clock-solar-storage-project/);
corroborated by Saur Energy and The National).

What makes the project unusual isn't the solar panels — 5.2 gigawatts is
large but no longer exotic — it's the batteries. The storage is sized to
soak up daytime generation and discharge it through the night, so the
plant can guarantee a full gigawatt of continuous, round-the-clock power
rather than the familiar solar pattern of a strong midday peak and
nothing after dusk. Masdar and EWEC are describing it as the largest and
most technologically advanced project of its kind to reach this stage.
"This financial close demonstrates the confidence of the international
banking community not only in a landmark project but also in Masdar's
financial strength," said Mazin Khan, the company's chief financial
officer.

That distinction — round-the-clock renewable power rather than
intermittent renewable power — is the one that matters for grid
operators. Solar and wind have gotten cheap enough to out-compete fossil
generation on price in much of the world; what they haven't done, at
this scale, is prove they can substitute for the "baseload" role that
coal, gas, and nuclear plants have always played, providing power whether
or not the weather cooperates. A 5.2 GW solar field bundled with enough
battery capacity to flatten its output into a steady, predictable
gigawatt is a direct attempt at closing that gap — using batteries, not
a backup gas turbine, to do the smoothing.

The project broke ground in October 2025 and is targeting commercial
operation in 2027. Thirteen banks agreeing to underwrite $5.1 billion of
it is itself a data point: gigascale solar-plus-storage has moved from
demonstration project to something a banking consortium will finance on
conventional terms, which is usually the signal that a technology has
crossed from promising to bankable. If it performs as designed, it will
be one of the largest working proofs yet that a renewable plant can be
asked to behave like a power plant that never stops — not just on the
sunniest afternoon of the year, but every night in between.
