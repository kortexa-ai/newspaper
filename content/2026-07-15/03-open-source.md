Blender, the free and open-source 3D suite that quietly powers a growing
share of the world's animated film, game, and product-design work,
released version 5.2 as its newest Long-Term Support edition on July 14 —
meaning the Blender Foundation commits to backporting fixes for it until
July 2028, regardless of how many feature releases ship in between
([9to5Linux](https://9to5linux.com/blender-5-2-lts-promises-new-fill-tool-and-thin-wall-mode-beta-out-now); [Tux Machines](https://news.tuxmachines.org/n/2026/07/14/Blender_5_2_LTS_Released_with_New_Fill_Tool_and_Thin_Wall_Mode_.shtml)).

The headline addition is a rebuilt Fill tool: it now constructs exact
geometry directly from boundary strokes, with automatic gap detection and
inverse filling, and runs faster regardless of how far you've zoomed in.
Alongside it comes a new Mesh Bevel node, giving procedural, node-based
control over edges and vertices that riggers and modelers have been asking
for since geometry nodes first shipped. The release also adds Geometry
bundles, a Sample Sound node that can pull frequency data straight out of
an audio track for reactive animation, and support for physics simulations
built entirely out of geometry nodes.

None of this requires a license fee, a subscription, or a seat count —
Blender has been free since 2002, sustained by a foundation, corporate
backers, and volunteer developers. An LTS release like this one is the
project's way of telling studios and hobbyists alike that the tool under
their pipeline isn't going to shift under them for two years, even as the
faster-moving feature branches keep pushing ahead.
