For one month, in one of the world's largest power grids, the sun did more
work than any other source of electricity. Solar panels generated 52
terawatt-hours across the European Union in June 2026 — 25% of the bloc's
total electricity, and enough to make solar the single largest contributor
to the grid, ahead of nuclear (21%), gas (15%), wind (14%), hydro (12%), and
coal, which has shrunk to just 8% ([CleanTechnica](https://cleantechnica.com/2026/07/14/25-of-eu-power-came-from-solar-for-the-first-time-in-june/); [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/2026/07/14/solar-generated-record-25-of-eu-power-in-june-with-germany-spain-and-poland-leading-the-ra)).

The figures, drawn from Ember's Monthly Electricity Data, mark the second
record in as many months: June's 52 TWh beat May's previous high of 47 TWh
(23%). "Solar's rise has been truly stratospheric, beating prediction after
prediction," said Chris Rosslowe, a senior energy analyst at Ember, the
energy think tank that compiles the EU-wide numbers.

The national breakdowns are where the story gets concrete. Spain drew more
than a third of its power from solar in June — 34%, a first for the
country. Germany, not exactly famous for sunshine, went from a third in May
(33%) to 36% in June. Poland, which has built out solar capacity
aggressively in recent years, reached nearly a quarter of its electricity
(24%) from panels alone. None of these are small, sun-drenched economies
riding a fluke month — they're three of the EU's biggest, most
industrialized grids, all pulling a quarter or more of their power from
photovoltaics at the same time.

What's behind it is less a single breakthrough than the compounding of a
decade of ordinary ones: solar capacity in the EU has grown by more than
20% a year between 2021 and 2025, with 65.1 gigawatts of new panels
installed in 2025 alone. Cheaper modules, faster permitting in several
member states, and a buildout of battery storage to smooth out solar's
daytime peak have turned steady annual growth into a source that can now,
for a summer month at least, out-produce everything else on the grid at
once.

It's a monthly record, not a permanent state of affairs — solar's share
will fall back this winter as daylight hours shrink, and June's numbers
benefit from the seasonal peak the same way a ski resort's best month isn't
July. But the EU's grid has now demonstrated, in practice rather than in a
model, that a modern industrial economy can run a quarter of itself on
sunlight without the lights flickering. Every summer from here amounts to a
test of how much higher that number can go before the winter reset — and on
the evidence of the last two months, the answer keeps being: higher than
last time.
