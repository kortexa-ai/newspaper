title: A solar cell just hit 35.5% efficiency, independently checked
kicker: Climate

summary: LONGi's silicon-perovskite tandem cell set a new certified efficiency record, edging closer to a ceiling far above what today's rooftop panels can reach.

Ordinary silicon solar cells are boxed in by physics: no matter how good
the engineering gets, a single-layer silicon cell tops out around 29%
efficiency, a hard ceiling known as the Shockley-Queisser limit. Stacking
a second, different light-absorbing layer on top — a tandem cell — is
one of the few ways around that wall, and LONGi, China's largest solar
manufacturer, just pushed that approach to a new record.

The company's crystalline silicon-perovskite tandem cell reached 35.5%
conversion efficiency, independently certified by the European Solar
Test Installation, part of the EU's Joint Research Centre
([LONGi](https://www.longi.com/en/news/crystalline-silicon-perovskite-tandem-solar-cell-new-world-efficiency-2026/);
corroborated by
[pv magazine](https://www.pv-magazine.com/2026/07/15/longi-sets-new-world-record-with-35-5-efficient-perovskite-silicon-tandem-cell/)
and
[PR Newswire](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/35-5-longi-once-again-breaks-world-record-for-crystalline-silicon-perovskite-tandem-solar-cell-efficiency-302827280.html)),
announced July 14 at the Solar and Energy Storage Innovation Conference.
It's the latest step in a steady climb for LONGi's tandem program, up
from 34.6% a year earlier, on a technology whose theoretical ceiling
sits around 43%.

The company is upfront about the gap between lab result and rooftop
reality: on cells sized closer to what a factory would actually
produce, efficiency drops to about 34%, and full modules — the panels
that would go on a roof — currently reach only around 29 to 31%. LONGi
says it has no active mass-production timeline for the tandem cells.
That's the honest caveat behind every record like this one: it's a
laboratory proof that the ceiling keeps moving, not a panel you can buy
yet. But every commercial silicon panel on the market today traces its
lineage back to lab records exactly like this one, set a few years
before the technology showed up on anyone's roof.
