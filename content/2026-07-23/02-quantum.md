title: A German startup ships quantum computers that plug into a wall outlet
kicker: Science
author: The Editors
summary: SAXON Q's diamond-based processors run at room temperature with no cryogenics, and its earlier systems have already logged a year of continuous operation at two German research institutes.
order: 2
---

Most quantum computers need a small industrial plant to run: dilution
refrigerators chilling qubits to near absolute zero, vibration-isolated
rooms, and specialists to keep it all stable. SAXON Q, a spinout of
Leipzig University, launched two commercial systems this week that skip
all of that. The SXQ128 and SXQ512 — 128 and 512 qubits respectively —
run at room temperature, fit in a standard server rack, and plug into a
normal outlet
([The Quantum Insider](https://thequantuminsider.com/2026/07/21/saxon-q-diamond-nv-center-quantum-computers/);
corroborated by
[Quantum Computing Report](https://quantumcomputingreport.com/saxon-q-launches-100-qubit-diamond-based-room-temperature-quantum-computers/)
and [HPCwire](https://www.hpcwire.com/off-the-wire/saxon-q-brings-room-temperature-diamond-quantum-computing-to-commercial-market/)).

The qubits are nitrogen-vacancy centers — single-atom defects engineered
into diamond — a technique physicists have chased for thirty years without
being able to manufacture past ten qubits reliably. SAXON Q says it solved
that manufacturing bottleneck: the new systems claim gate fidelities up to
99.92% and run 6 to 10 times more energy-efficiently than GPU-based
approaches, all without cryogenic cooling.

"For thirty years, NV-center quantum computing was a question of
manufacturing," said CEO Marius Grundmann, a Leipzig University physics
professor. "We solved that problem. The SXQ128 and SXQ512 are quantum
computers that operate the way a computer should: reliably, continuously
and without a team of specialists to keep it running."

The claims aren't just marketing copy. An earlier-generation SAXON Q
system has been running at the Fraunhofer Institute for Machine Tools and
Forming Technology since June 2025, used for materials-processing and
robotics optimization. "The system has operated at room temperature
continuously since installation — and has exceeded the gate fidelity
specifications we outlined in the tender," said Albrecht Hänel, the
institute's head of digital product twin. A separate system is deployed
at the German Aerospace Center.

The SXQ128 is orderable now, with delivery in about three months; the
SXQ512 ships starting in the second quarter of 2027. Neither will rival
the biggest superconducting or trapped-ion machines on raw qubit count.
But a quantum computer that survives being shipped to a factory floor and
left running unattended is its own kind of milestone — the difference
between a lab demo and a tool.
