South Carolina has finished a five-year effort to bring high-speed
internet to homes the market had passed over, officials announced this
week: 21,644 rural housing units across 21 underserved counties now have
broadband access that didn't exist before
([South Carolina Public Radio](https://www.southcarolinapublicradio.org/sc-news/2026-07-15/state-initiative-brings-high-speed-internet-to-over-21-000-rural-south-carolina-homes)).

The buildout, run through the state's Broadband Office within the Office
of Regulatory Staff, drew $30 million from a $65 million rural
development fund the state legislature set aside back in 2019. Seventeen
different internet service providers did the actual construction,
laying 2,041 miles of new fiber-optic cable at an average cost of $1,386
per household — physical work that wrapped in March 2026, with this
month's announcement marking the program's formal completion.

There's nothing flashy about a fiber build: it's trenches, spliced
cable, and years of unglamorous scheduling across dozens of small
providers and hundreds of miles of rural road. But it's the kind of
infrastructure that determines whether a household can work remotely,
attend school online, or run a small business — options that simply
don't exist without a wire (or now, increasingly, without fiber
specifically). The remaining money from the original 2019 allocation is
now funding 31 additional local modernization projects under the
state's Rural Initiative Grant Program, so the fund keeps working even
after this chapter closes.
