---
title: Two new ways to break the unbreakable chemical bond
kicker: Climate
author: The Editors
summary: German researchers found that collapsing vapor bubbles and cold plasma can both tear apart PFAS "forever chemicals," destroying rather than just filtering them.
order: 3
---

PFAS compounds earned the nickname "forever chemicals" honestly: the
carbon-fluorine bond holding them together is one of the strongest in
organic chemistry, which is why they resist breaking down in water,
soil, or the human body. Most treatment methods today just move the
problem — filtering PFAS out of water and concentrating it somewhere
else. Researchers at Helmholtz-Zentrum Dresden-Rossendorf have been
testing two methods that actually break the bond.

The first, hydrodynamic cavitation, forces water through a system that
generates collapsing vapor bubbles; each collapse briefly produces
temperatures in the thousands of degrees Celsius and a burst of
reactive hydroxyl radicals, which degraded roughly 37 percent of
dissolved PFOS molecules in testing, with the team aiming for 80
percent and higher. The second, cold atmospheric plasma with gas
dispersion, worked faster still, nearly eliminating both long- and
short-chain PFAS and releasing about 35 percent of the compounds' bound
fluorine as harmless fluoride salts — though it uses more energy and
leaves byproducts the team is still characterizing. Analytical
confirmation came from the Helmholtz Centre for Environmental Research.
The results published July 19 in *Scientific Reports* and *Chemical
Engineering Journal Advances*
([ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260718010147.htm)).

Neither method is ready to scale to a municipal water plant tomorrow.
But having two independent, mechanistically different ways to actually
destroy PFAS — rather than just relocate it — gives water utilities
options where, until recently, they had none.
