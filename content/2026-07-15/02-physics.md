title: A 200-year-old shadow trick just made exotic light patterns cheap to create
kicker: Science
author: The Editors
summary: Singapore researchers used the Poisson spot — a classic 19th-century optics demonstration — to generate optical skyrmions without the costly engineered materials the technique used to require.
order: 2
---

In 1818, a physicist trying to disprove the wave theory of light
accidentally proved it: shine a laser at a small disc, and rather than a
clean shadow, a bright spot appears dead center, where diffracted light
waves reconverge. It's been a staple of physics classrooms ever since. Now
a team at Nanyang Technological University in Singapore has put that
200-year-old party trick to work building some of the more exotic
structures in modern optics.

Led by Assistant Professor Shen Yijie, the team used the Poisson spot setup
to generate optical skyrmions — tiny, stable, swirling configurations in
the properties of light, shaped a bit like the spikes of a hedgehog, that
physicists have flagged as promising carriers for future data storage,
communications, and computing. Producing them has typically required
expensive, purpose-engineered metamaterials. Shen's team got the same
result with a laser and a circular disc, and in the process generated four
related skyrmion types at once — spin, Stokes, electric-field, and
magnetic-field skyrmions — letting researchers compare topological
structures within a single beam of light. The work was published in
*Optica* on June 23, 2026 ([NTU Singapore](https://www.ntu.edu.sg/news/detail/ntu-singapore-scientists-create-optical-skyrmions-using-a-two-century-old-light-phenomenon); [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260713000755.htm)).

"This could make optical skyrmions much more accessible to researchers,"
the team noted. "By lowering the technical barrier to creating and
studying them, the method opens up new possibilities for scientists to
study how they could be used in future optical, materials and computing
research." It's a reminder that some of the more useful hardware in
tomorrow's photonics labs won't come from a new material at all — just a
closer look at an effect that's been sitting in textbooks since before
electricity was in most homes.
