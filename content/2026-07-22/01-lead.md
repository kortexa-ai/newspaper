Satellites in geostationary orbit don't break down so much as run out of gas.
The hardware is often still fine; it's the maneuvering fuel that runs dry,
and once it does, a billion-dollar spacecraft becomes 22,000 miles of
expensive junk with nothing wrong with it. On Tuesday, a mission years in
the making launched to start fixing that.

A SpaceX Falcon 9 lifted off from Cape Canaveral at 5:15 p.m. EDT carrying
Northrop Grumman's Mission Robotic Vehicle (MRV) and three Mission Extension
Pods, on the last flight of a Falcon 9 first-stage booster that had flown 31
times before
([Spaceflight Now](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/07/21/live-coverage-spacex-to-launch-novel-geosynchronous-robotic-servicing-satellite-on-decade-long-mission/);
corroborated by
[Satellite Today](https://www.satellitetoday.com/launch/2026/07/21/spacex-launches-northrop-grummans-first-mission-robotic-vehicle/)
and [Washington Times](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2026/jul/21/mission-launches-extend-life-gas-communication-satellites/)).
The MRV is a 3,000-kilogram spacecraft built to do something no American
vehicle has done commercially before: grab hold of another satellite it
wasn't designed to dock with, and fix it.

The idea traces back to a DARPA program called RSGS — Robotic Servicing of
Geosynchronous Satellites — which challenged engineers to build a robot
capable of servicing essentially any satellite already in orbit, not just
ones built with a docking port in mind. The Naval Research Laboratory
designed the answer: two three-meter, seven-degrees-of-freedom robotic arms
with interchangeable tools, guided by more than twenty situational-awareness
cameras. Northrop Grumman built the spacecraft around that payload.

The trick is where the arms grab. Every satellite that reaches orbit rides
there on a rocket, which means every satellite has a sturdy ring built to
survive launch loads — the "launch vehicle interface plane." It's structural,
it's standardized enough to be predictable, and it was never meant to be
touched again once the satellite separated from its rocket. "Every satellite
got to space on a rocket," said Glen Henshaw, NRL's lead space roboticist.
That one fact turned out to be the key: target that ring, and the arms can
safely grip almost any spacecraft without going near its instruments.

The MRV won't do anything right away. It launched into a geostationary
transfer orbit and will spend roughly a year climbing to its working orbit
36,000 kilometers up on solar-electric propulsion, the same slow-and-steady
approach its three Mission Extension Pods will follow independently. Once
everyone arrives, the plan is methodical: the MRV picks up a pod, carries it
to a client satellite — Optus and SES are both signed up — and installs it.
Each pod is essentially a fuel tank with a thruster, good for up to eight
years of additional life once attached. Northrop Grumman says a single MRV,
resupplied over time, could eventually extend the working life of up to
thirty satellites.

There's a tidy piece of forward design in the spacecraft too: the MRV
carries a Passive Refueling Module, the first such system approved by the
U.S. Space Force, which means the mechanic itself can eventually be
refueled rather than retired. For an industry that has spent decades
treating orbital hardware as disposable once its tanks run dry, that's the
quieter headline — not one satellite getting a second life, but the
beginning of infrastructure that assumes satellites should get one at all.
