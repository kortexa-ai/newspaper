title: A rocket booster just flew for the 600th time
kicker: Space
author: The Editors
summary: SpaceX reused a Falcon 9 first stage for the 600th time on July 14, on a booster now flying its 28th mission — evidence that rocket reuse has become routine infrastructure, not a stunt.

---

At 5:10 a.m. Eastern on July 14, a Falcon 9 lifted off from Cape Canaveral
carrying 29 Starlink satellites, on a first-stage booster — tail number
B1080 — making its 28th flight. About eight and a half minutes later, that
booster landed itself on the droneship "A Shortfall of Gravitas" in the
Atlantic, the 161st landing on that particular vessel and the 600th time a
previously flown Falcon booster has been reused since SpaceX first pulled
the trick off in 2017 ([Space.com](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/launches-spacecraft/spacex-starlink-15-14-b1093-vsfb-ocisly-10-45-b1080-ccsfs-asog); [Spaceflight Now](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/07/13/live-coverage-spacex-to-launch-a-flight-proven-falcon-booster-for-600th-time/)).

B1080 has had a varied career: two Axiom Space private astronaut missions,
the launch of ESA's Euclid space telescope, a Northrop Grumman cargo run to
the International Space Station, and now its 28th trip to orbit carrying
broadband satellites. It launched as half of a coast-to-coast doubleheader
— a second Falcon 9 lifted off from Vandenberg Space Force Base in
California just hours earlier, also landing successfully, bringing SpaceX's
career total to 638 booster landings.

The milestone isn't a single dramatic breakthrough so much as the
accumulation of one: a fleet of rocket boosters now being inspected,
refurbished, and reflown at something like commercial airline cadence,
rather than treated as disposable hardware. That's the quiet infrastructure
question the industry argued about for most of the 2010s — not whether a
booster could land, but whether a fleet of them could be flown over and
over without the reliability or cost advantages evaporating. Six hundred
reused flights in, the answer looks settled.
