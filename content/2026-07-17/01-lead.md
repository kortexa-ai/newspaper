Some discoveries come from a flash of new data. This one came from finally
noticing something that had been there all along.

Beta Pictoris is one of astronomy's favorite stars — a young system, still
wrapped in the dusty disk that planets form from, and home to the first
exoplanet ever directly photographed. Astronomers have been watching it
closely for decades. So when a team using the European Southern
Observatory's Very Large Telescope in Chile spotted a faint smudge near the
star this year, the natural next step was to check whether anyone had seen
it before.

They had — repeatedly, without realizing it. Digging back through eleven
years of archival images, including earlier observations from the VLT's
SPHERE instrument, the team led by Ben Sutlieff of the University of
Edinburgh and Markus Bonse of ESO found the same faint object showing up
again and again, misread as noise or overlooked entirely. It is now
confirmed as Beta Pictoris d, a gas giant roughly 2.4 times the mass of
Jupiter, orbiting its star at a distance of about 63 light-years from
Earth. At roughly a hundred times fainter than its sibling planet Beta
Pictoris b, it is the faintest planet ever directly imaged from the ground
([ESO](https://www.eso.org/public/news/eso2609/)).

"Planet d, it seems, has been playing a game of hide-and-seek with us for
over a decade," said Jayne Birkby of the University of Oxford, one of more
than ninety co-authors on the discovery paper, published July 15 in *The
Astrophysical Journal Letters*.

The find got an unusually fast second opinion. A separate team led by
Aidan Gibbs at the University of California independently spotted the same
planet using NASA's James Webb Space Telescope, arriving at the discovery
through an entirely different instrument at almost the same time. Two
telescopes, two teams, one answer — about as clean a confirmation as
observational astronomy gets.

The result also puts Beta Pictoris in rare company: it is now only the
second planetary system, after HR 8799, in which more than two planets
have been directly photographed rather than inferred from a star's wobble
or a dimming light curve. That matters because directly imaged planets are
exactly what let astronomers actually study a world's atmosphere and
composition, rather than just its existence.

There's a quieter lesson here too. The planet wasn't found by a bigger
telescope or a longer exposure — it was found by someone going back and
looking harder at data the field already had. As archives from
instruments like SPHERE keep accumulating, astronomers are increasingly
finding that some of the universe's answers were already sitting in the
files, waiting to be asked about the right way.
