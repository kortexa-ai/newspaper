For decades, the search for a habitable world has run into the same wall.
Astronomers can find rocky planets in the right orbit around the right kind
of star — not too hot, not too cold, the band where water could stay liquid
on a surface. What they couldn't confirm was whether any of those planets
had held on to an atmosphere at all, since the same small, dim red dwarf
stars that make rocky planets easy to spot also blast them with radiation
that can strip a thin atmosphere away. This week, that wall came down.

A team led by Collin Cherubim, who recently completed his Ph.D. in Earth
and Planetary Sciences at Harvard, confirmed the first atmosphere ever
detected on a rocky planet sitting in its star's habitable zone. The
target was LHS 1140 b, a super-Earth about 48 light-years from us, orbiting
the red dwarf LHS 1140 — a planet first discovered by co-author Jason
Dittmann, now at the University of Florida
([Center for Astrophysics | Harvard & Smithsonian](https://www.cfa.harvard.edu/news/first-atmosphere-detected-habitable-zone-rocky-world);
corroborated by [EurekAlert!](https://www.eurekalert.org/news-releases/1135901)
and [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/07/260721000825.htm)).
The findings were published in *Science* on July 16.

The evidence came from helium, not the usual suspects like carbon dioxide
or water vapor. Using the WINERED spectrograph on the Magellan Clay
telescope in Chile, the team caught LHS 1140 b during a rare alignment, as
it and another planet in the system both crossed in front of their star.
Starlight filtering through the planet's upper atmosphere during that
transit revealed helium escaping into space — a signature Cherubim had
predicted before the observation even happened, having modeled which of
the known habitable-zone rocky worlds would most likely still be holding
a helium-rich envelope. The detection matched the prediction closely
enough that the team calls it statistically solid.

"An atmosphere is essential for a planet to support life as we know it,"
Cherubim said. "This is the first time anyone has found an atmosphere on
a rocky planet in the habitable zone of another star."

What makes the find more than a one-off is the timescale. Researchers
estimate the atmosphere could have persisted for more than three billion
years — long enough, in principle, for whatever chemistry or geology
happens beneath it to matter. It isn't confirmation of oceans or life;
helium alone doesn't tell you that. What it tells you is that the
planet has weathered billions of years of stellar radiation without
losing its air, which is the precondition every other question about
habitability depends on.

The team's next targets are the harder ones: hunting for water vapor and
carbon dioxide deeper in the atmosphere, which would start to say something
about whether the surface below could hold liquid water. Cherubim's
prediction model, now validated once, will also be pointed at other
habitable-zone rocky planets to see which ones are worth the telescope
time. LHS 1140 b was a single needle found in a very large haystack. The
real payoff is that astronomers now have a method for finding the next one.
