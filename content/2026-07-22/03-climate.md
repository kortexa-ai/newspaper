---
title: The Amazon just had its quietest fire season in 41 years
kicker: Climate
author: The Editors
summary: A MapBiomas report released this week found fire-scarred land across Brazil down 58% in 2025, with the Amazon posting its smallest burned area on record.
order: 3
---

A year after fires tore across the Amazon at a scale not seen in decades,
the numbers went the other way. A new report from the Brazilian monitoring
network MapBiomas, released this week, found that 3.1 million hectares
burned in the Amazon in 2025 — the smallest area since satellite records
began in 1985, and an 80% drop from 2024. Fire-scarred land across all of
Brazil fell 58%, to 12.7 million hectares, the lowest national total since
2018
([phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2026-07-brazil-amazon-historic.html);
corroborated by
[Carbon Pulse](https://carbon-pulse.com/533422/) and
[Bangkok Post](https://www.bangkokpost.com/world/3289330/fires-in-brazils-amazon-dropped-to-historic-low-in-2025)).

Geographer Ane Alencar, who coordinated the report, called the swing "out
of the ordinary." Part of it was luck: a rainy season that arrived late
blocked the window farmers usually use to burn land for clearing. Part of
it was policy working as intended — deforestation eased last year, which
meant less cleared, dried vegetation sitting around as fuel. And part of it,
Alencar said, was people: after living through 2024's fires, communities
were quicker to catch and contain new outbreaks before they spread.

The report isn't an all-clear. The Cerrado, Brazil's savanna region, kept
burning at rates similar to prior years, and Alencar flagged the possible
arrival of a "super El Niño" that could dry things out again in 2026. But
after a fire season that made global headlines for the worst reasons, a
year in which the numbers simply got better — not spun, not offset, just
better — is itself the story.
