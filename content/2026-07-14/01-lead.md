Omega Centauri is the biggest ball of stars in our galaxy's neighborhood —
ten million of them, bound together roughly 18,000 light-years away, some
of the oldest stars anyone has found. For decades, models of how such
clusters evolve predicted it should be carrying around 10,000 stellar-mass
black holes, the crushed remains of its most massive stars. Direct evidence
of even one had stayed stubbornly out of reach.

On Monday, a team led by Matthew Whitaker at the University of Utah
announced they'd found it — not by seeing the black hole, which is
impossible by definition, but by watching what it does to its neighbor.
Combing through 23 years of archival Hubble Space Telescope images
(2002–2025) and adding fresh near-infrared observations from the James
Webb Space Telescope, the team tracked the motion of an ordinary
main-sequence star as it was tugged, almost imperceptibly, around an
invisible companion ([NASA](https://science.nasa.gov/missions/hubble/nasas-hubble-discovers-first-of-star-clusters-missing-black-holes/), [University of Utah](https://attheu.utah.edu/facultystaff/nasas-hubble-discovers-first-of-star-clusters-missing-black-holes/)).

"With Hubble and Webb data, we were able to see the motion of the visible
main sequence star that is part of this binary," Whitaker said. "The
precision of these measurements is incredible, down to a fraction of a
pixel on Hubble and Webb's detectors. It would not have been possible to
find this black hole without these two space telescopes."

The numbers that fall out of that wobble are striking. The visible star
weighs 0.78 times the mass of our sun; its hidden partner, the team
calculates, weighs 4.46 solar masses — too heavy to be a neutron star, but
lighter than theory says a black hole born in a metal-poor cluster like
Omega Centauri ought to be. Stranger still is the orbit: the pair circle
each other once every 94 years, at a distance of roughly 31 astronomical
units, on a stretched, eccentric path. That makes it the longest-period
black hole binary ever documented — most known systems complete an orbit
in hours or days, not decades (paper: [Whitaker et al., 2026, ApJL, via arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18350)).

"While we already knew that the star was 0.78 solar masses, we can now
calculate the black hole's mass, which is 4.46 solar masses and therefore
too heavy to be a neutron star," said Anil Seth, a University of Utah
professor and co-author. "However, its mass is much lower than would be
expected in a metal-poor environment like Omega Centauri. This is
surprising and exciting." Seth added that pinning down how black holes
form and pair off inside clusters like this one matters well beyond the
cluster itself: it feeds directly into how astronomers interpret the
gravitational-wave signals now routinely picked up from merging black
holes across the universe.

The find, catalogued as oMEGACat BH-2, is a proof of concept as much as a
discovery — astrometry, the exacting business of measuring tiny stellar
motions over long stretches of time, has just shown it can pull hidden
black holes out of even the most crowded stellar environments. "With
Hubble and Webb, we can continue to look at Omega Centauri and expand our
search for similar systems within other clusters," Whitaker said. Somewhere
in that cluster, in other words, nine thousand, nine hundred and
ninety-nine more are still waiting to be caught the same way.
