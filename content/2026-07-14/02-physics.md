---
title: Physicists recreate a black hole's power trick, without anything spinning
kicker: Science
author: The Editors
summary: A ring of electronic resonators at CUNY reproduced, on a lab bench, the amplification mechanism a spinning black hole is theorized to use to hand energy to passing waves.
order: 2
---

In 1971, Roger Penrose showed that a rotating black hole carries spare
energy in its ergosphere — the whirling region just outside its event
horizon — and that, in principle, an object falling through the right way
could extract some of it. Yakov Zel'dovich soon proposed a wave version of
the same trick: send a wave in with the right twist, and it comes back out
amplified, having stolen energy from the hole's rotation. Both ideas have
stood for more than fifty years without a direct experimental test, for the
obvious reason that nobody can spin anything in a lab fast enough to
matter.

A team at the CUNY Graduate Center's Advanced Science Research Center,
led by physicist Andrea Alù, found a way around that limit by not spinning
anything at all. Reporting in *Nature* on July 8, they built a ring-shaped
network of electronic resonators whose properties are switched on a
precisely timed sequence, creating a traveling pattern that looks, to an
incoming wave, exactly like ultrafast rotation — a "synthetic" spin that
can move faster than any physical object could ([CUNY ASRC](https://asrc.gc.cuny.edu/headlines/2026/07/a-black-hole-theory-comes-to-life-in-the-lab/), [EurekAlert](https://www.eurekalert.org/news-releases/1135209)).

When they sent radio waves with the right rotational character into the
ring, the waves came out amplified, having drawn energy from the
synthetic rotation — reproducing, on a circuit board, the essential
physics of the Penrose-Zel'dovich process. "Our approach facilitates a new
method of wave-matter interaction in which waves with selected rotational
properties extract energy from synthetic time-engineered rotation,
producing a form of broadband selective amplification," Alù said. Lead
author Hadiseh Nasari called it a way of moving "ideas about extreme
rotational dynamics from theory to practice," creating, in her words, "a
versatile experimental platform for exploring a broad range of phenomena."

Nobody is claiming a black hole in a box. What the CUNY team has built is
a validated analogue — a device that borrows a black hole's mathematics to
do something practical, amplifying waves without adding external power to
them directly. Work funded by the Department of Defense, the National
Science Foundation, and the Simons Foundation now has a tabletop
instrument for testing predictions that, until last week, existed only on
paper.
