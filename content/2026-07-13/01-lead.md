title: A net over the ocean caught a rocket, and the price of space fell again

On Friday, a Long March 10B lifted off from the Hainan International
Commercial Aerospace Launch Center, put a satellite into its planned
orbit, and then did the part that made history: about six minutes
after stage separation, its first-stage booster descended onto an
offshore platform more than 300 kilometers away and was caught —
not on landing legs, not by mechanical arms, but by hooks settling
into a purpose-built net. It is the first net-based sea capture of
a rocket booster anywhere, and it makes China the second nation ever
to recover an orbital-class first stage.

Two details make this more than a flag-planting exercise. First, it
worked on the vehicle's maiden flight — a clean first-attempt booster
recovery is something even the current leaders in reusability never
managed. Second, the approach is genuinely different. SpaceX's
Falcon 9 lands on legs; Starship is caught by tower arms. A net at
sea is a third answer to the same question, and every new working
answer expands what the whole field knows.

The reason to cheer this from anywhere on Earth is arithmetic, not
allegiance. Reusability is the single biggest lever on the cost of
reaching orbit, and cost is the gate in front of everything else we
want from space: denser scientific constellations, cheaper Earth
observation for farmers and firefighters, more research payloads
that a university can actually afford. When one organization holds a
monopoly on recovering boosters, that lever moves at one
organization's pace. As of Friday, it moves at two nations' pace —
and the engineers in Hainan say they intend to refly this booster by
the end of the year.

Competition in launch has a short and encouraging history: prices
fall, cadence rises, and capabilities that were exotic a decade ago
become line items. A decade from now, the picture of a booster
hanging in a net off the Chinese coast may look like one of those
quiet inflection points — the moment the club of reusable rocketry
stopped being a club of one.
