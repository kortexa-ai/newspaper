title: A new undersea cable just tied Portugal and the US closer together
kicker: Infrastructure
author: The Editors
summary: Google's Nuvem cable went into service this week, adding a 7,000-kilometer transatlantic route via Bermuda and the Azores to a network that's grown steadily more resilient.
order: 3
---

The internet's transatlantic backbone got a little sturdier this week.
Google's Nuvem subsea cable — Portuguese for "cloud" — was connected and
inaugurated at a ceremony at Lisbon's Oceanarium, linking Myrtle Beach,
South Carolina, to Sines, Portugal, with intermediate landing points in
Bermuda and the Azores
([The Portugal News](https://www.theportugalnews.com/news/2026-07-21/google-launches-submarine-cable-that-connects-portugal-to-the-united-states/1058610);
corroborated by
[Submarine Networks](https://www.submarinenetworks.com/en/systems/trans-atlantic/nuvem/google-s-nuvem-subsea-cable-lands-in-myrtle-beach)
and the original
[Google Cloud announcement](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/infrastructure/introducing-the-nuvem-subsea-cable)).
Officials at the ceremony described it as a step in Portugal's positioning
as "Europe's digital gateway."

The system runs roughly 7,000 kilometers and carries 16 fiber pairs, part
of a wider build-out Google has pursued since Nuvem was first announced
in 2023. "Nuvem will add capacity, increase reliability, and decrease
latency for Google users and Google Cloud customers around the world,"
said Brian Quigley, the company's VP of Global Network Infrastructure,
at the time of the announcement. Alongside two earlier cables, Firmina
(landing in South Carolina, reaching Argentina, Brazil, and Uruguay) and
Equiano (Portugal to West and Southern Africa), Nuvem completes a set of
routes Quigley called "the underwater roots that strengthen the
intercontinental network lattice."

It's easy to treat undersea cables as invisible infrastructure that only
makes news when one breaks. The more useful way to see this one: the vast
majority of intercontinental data still travels through cables like it,
and adding a new, geographically distinct route is what keeps a fishing
trawler's anchor or an earthquake off Newfoundland from becoming an
outage felt on both continents. This is Google's second cable to land in Portugal, with
a third, Cape Sol, planned for the Azores in 2027 — a bet that the
Atlantic's data traffic keeps growing, and that redundancy is worth
building ahead of it.
